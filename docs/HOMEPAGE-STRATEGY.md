# The Alara Homepage — First-Principles Strategy

> Brief: not a redesign of the current site, but the design of *the single best home health
> website in the United States* for a federal-benefits specialist. Standard: "If Alara became
> the most trusted federal-benefits home-care company in America, what would its website look like?"
>
> Written as: Apple creative direction · Aman design · Stripe UX · healthcare CRO · Mayo IA ·
> Bloomberg editorial · a CMS surveyor judging legitimacy · a federal-benefits expert.

## PART 1 — FIRST-PRINCIPLES ANALYSIS

### 1.1 The business, honestly

Alara is a **nurse-owned, DON-led home health agency in Clark County, Nevada** that is *specialized*,
not general. Revenue and referrals come from federal-benefit programs most agencies can't bill or
don't understand: **EEOICPA / White Card** (Acentra/WCMBP, DOL), **OWCP / FECA** (federal & postal
workers), **VA Community Care** (TriWest, Region 4), plus **Medicare Part A** seniors.

The moat is not "compassion" (every agency claims it) and not "luxury" (a feeling, not a defensible
asset). The moat is **federal-benefits mastery + clinical legitimacy**:
- knowing the White Card authorization path, consequential-condition pathway, and how to get DOL
  claims paid in 15–28 days;
- a Director of Nursing who reviews every start-of-care OASIS before submission;
- being one of the few CCN/TriWest and EEOICPA-fluent agencies in the market.

**Strategic conclusion:** the website's job is to make that moat *visible and believable in seconds*,
then route each visitor to the right next step. The brand line that owns the category:
**"The federal-benefits home-care authority."**

### 1.2 The six visitors (and what each actually needs)

| Visitor | Emotional state | Decisive question | What converts them |
|---|---|---|---|
| **White Card patient / adult child** | Overwhelmed, protective, skeptical of "too good to be true" | "Is this benefit real, what does it cover, and is this agency legitimate?" | Plain-language proof + "no out-of-pocket cost" + named clinician + accreditation |
| **Federal / postal worker (OWCP/FECA)** | Frustrated by bureaucracy | "Will they handle the authorization so I don't have to?" | "We handle the paperwork" + authorization expertise |
| **Veteran (VA/TriWest)** | Wary of wait times | "Am I eligible, and can I avoid the VA backlog?" | CCN/TriWest provider status + "you don't need Medicare" + speed |
| **Referring physician** | Risk-averse, time-poor | "Will this make me look good and not create work?" | DON oversight, documentation quality, **one-hour response**, low-friction referral |
| **Case manager / DOL Resource Center / VA coordinator** | Accountable, compliance-minded | "Is this a legitimate, reliable partner I can stake my name on?" | CHAP, licensure, Medicare cert, named leadership, track record |
| **Adult child researching** | Anxious, comparison-shopping at midnight | "Are they real, and are they better than the others?" | Legitimacy signals + education + a human face |

Two buyers, one site: **patients/families** and **referrers**. The homepage must serve both
without diluting either — a dual-path conversion architecture, not a single funnel.

### 1.3 The competitive reality

- **Generic home health sites:** stock photos of smiling seniors, "compassionate, personalized
  care," a phone number, no specialization, no legitimacy proof, no education. Forgettable.
- **EEOICPA competitors (e.g., national White Card players):** transactional, salesy, "we come to
  you," national and impersonal — weak on *local clinical legitimacy* and *editorial authority*.
- **Premium healthcare (Mayo, Cleveland Clinic):** win on **audience-based IA**, **authoritative,
  sourced, clinician-reviewed content**, and restraint. They look like institutions.
- **Premium hospitality (Aman, Four Seasons):** win on **material restraint, space, photography,
  and confidence** — they never shout.
- **Editorial (Bloomberg):** wins on **typographic hierarchy and information authority** — dense
  where it matters, calm where it doesn't.

**The gap nobody fills:** a site that is *simultaneously* premium-restrained (Aman/Apple),
clinically legitimate (CMS/Mayo), authoritative on benefits (Bloomberg/expert), and conversion-
disciplined (Stripe/CRO). That intersection is Alara's to take.

### 1.4 The CMS-surveyor / legitimacy lens (the part most agencies fail)

In 5 seconds, a skeptical professional looks for: legal entity name, **state licensure**,
**accreditation (CHAP)**, **Medicare certification**, payer/network status (VA CCN/TriWest, EEOICPA),
a **named, credentialed clinical leader**, a real service area, and a real way to reach a human.
Beauty without these reads as marketing. **Legitimacy markers must be visible above the fold.**

## PART 2 — THE TEN QUESTIONS

**1. Emotional experience, first 10 seconds.**
Calm authority and relief. "These people are serious, legitimate clinicians who understand my exact
situation — and I am not alone in figuring this out." Not excitement, not warmth-for-its-own-sake.
The feeling of walking into an institution that was built for you.

**2. What the user must immediately understand.**
*Alara is a nurse-led, accredited home health agency that specializes in federal-benefit patients
(White Card/EEOICPA, OWCP, VA) in Southern Nevada — and most patients pay nothing.* And: *here is
exactly where I go next* (I'm a patient/family, or I'm a referrer).

**3. Above the fold.**
- Wordmark + audience/program navigation + phone (tap-to-call) + one primary CTA.
- Authority headline (positioning) + one proof line (nurse-owned · DON-led · the programs · no cost).
- **Credential bar** (CHAP · Nevada-licensed · Medicare-certified · VA CCN/TriWest · EEOICPA specialist).
- Dual CTA: *Find out if you qualify* (patient) and *Refer a patient* (referrer).
- The arch photograph as premium architectural support — framing, not the whole message.

**4. NOT above the fold.**
Founder story, long education, testimonials, service lists, the how-it-works steps, the Library
depth, FAQs. These are the *reward for scrolling*, sequenced to deepen trust — not crammed into the hero.

**5. Visual style for maximum trust + authority + premium.**
**Warm-neutral institutional minimalism.** The owned palette (espresso/taupe/greige/cream, no blue,
no clinical brightness). Architectural photography (the arch as a motif of permanence). Editorial
typographic hierarchy. Hairlines, never drop-shadows. Generous space. Credentials rendered as clean
typographic marks, not gaudy badges. Materials and restraint over decoration. *The luxury is the
confidence to say less.*

**6. Hero type: image, education, trust, or conversion?**
**Trust-led, conversion-anchored, image-supported.** Pure image = generic luxury, no legitimacy.
Pure conversion = SaaS landing page. The hero must assert *legitimacy + positioning* in words and
credentials, offer *two clear actions*, and use the photograph as the premium frame. Education lives
immediately below (the audience doors + the Library), not in the hero.

**7. Optimal homepage structure (top → bottom).** See PART 3 wireframe.

**8. Content blocks that should exist.**
Hero (trust+positioning+dual CTA) · Credential bar · Audience selector (the four federal-benefit
doors) · Why Alara (proof of expertise) · Understand your benefits (Navigator + Library) · Named
clinical leadership (the DON/founder) · How it works · For physicians & case managers (referral) ·
Proof (partners/outcomes, compliant) · Service area & legitimacy · Final dual CTA · Footer.

**9. What to remove entirely.**
Stock photography of any kind. "Compassionate, personalized care" boilerplate. Vague service grids
with no audience context. Platform-named nav items ("Benefit Navigator / Library / Trust & Sources"
as *top-level* nav — they're features, not destinations). Anything that reads as a marketing flyer.
Exclamation points. Any claim without a source or a credential behind it.

**10. If Aman + Apple + Mayo + Bloomberg built it.**
A calm, near-full-viewport hero with a confident editorial headline and a quiet credential line, set
against one extraordinary architectural photograph. Below: an audience selector that behaves like
Mayo's "find your path," rendered with Apple's spacing and Aman's restraint. Then a Bloomberg-grade
education layer (the Library) that proves authority, and a single, dignified portrait of the nurse
who runs the place. No carousels, no stock, no noise. It would look like an *institution that
happens to come to your home.*

### 3.2 Visual direction
- **Mood:** institutional calm. An organization with permanence (the arch), run by clinicians.
- **Palette:** espresso `#342E2E`, taupe `#817A7A`, greige `#D9D8D3`, mist `#E8E8E8`, paper `#FBFBFB`.
  One restrained accent only if ever needed; warmth comes from photography, not UI color. No blue.
- **Texture:** flat surfaces, 1px hairlines, no shadows. Space is the premium signal.
- **Credentials:** set as small-caps typographic marks on a hairline rule — never shiny badges.
- **Photography:** architectural (the arch = institution); any human imagery is real, documentary,
  warm-neutral graded, never stock. The founder portrait is dignified, eye-level, real.

### 3.3 Typography system
- **Display / wordmark / eyebrows:** geometric sans (the established Jost), light weight, wide tracking.
- **Headings + body:** humanist sans (Inter), weight 500 for headings, 400 body, senior-friendly 17px.
- **Editorial / Library layer (optional elevation):** a refined serif may be introduced *only* for
  long-form benefit guides to signal Bloomberg/Mayo authority — kept out of UI chrome.
- **Scale (modular ~1.25):** Display 40–52 · H1 32–40 · H2 22 · H3 18 · Body 17 · Small 15 · Caption
  13 · Eyebrow 11 (caps, .26em). Line-height 1.7 for reading. Two weights only.

### 3.4 Layout system
- 12-col grid, max content 1200px, long-form reading max 720–760px.
- Section rhythm `clamp(72px, 9vw, 140px)`; gutter `clamp(24px, 6vw, 80px)`.
- Editorial split for the hero and the feature bands; centered single-column for educational and
  trust sections. Alternating cream / mist / espresso section backgrounds for cadence.

### 3.5 Conversion architecture (Stripe/CRO discipline, trust-first)
- **Two primary paths, always available:** patient (*Find out if you qualify* / *Talk to a nurse*)
  and referrer (*Refer a patient*). Never make a referrer hunt.
- **Phone is a first-class CTA** (tap-to-call) — this audience calls. Phone-only intake reduces friction.
- **The Benefit Navigator is the soft-conversion engine** — an educational tool that qualifies intent
  without a hard ask (and quietly captures the structured-intent data moat).
- **Contextual CTA per section** (e.g., "Talk to a nurse about White Card wound care").
- **Trust before ask:** credentials and a named clinician precede every conversion moment.
- **Response promise** ("we answer within one hour") near every CTA. No urgency tactics, no popups.

### 3.6 Educational architecture (the authority engine)
- **Federal Benefits Library** (glossary + guides), **Benefit Navigator** (interactive), **FAQ hubs**,
  and **answer pages** — all clinician-reviewed, source-cited (DOL/VA/CMS), audience-organized (Mayo IA).
- Education is the differentiator: *no competitor teaches these benefits clearly.* It also feeds AEO
  (being the cited source) and the proprietary intent dataset.
- Homepage surfaces the Library as proof of authority, not as a top-nav utility.

### 3.7 Referral architecture
- Dedicated **/physicians** and **/refer**: "Your patients. Our paperwork." In-home assessment within
  48 hours, documentation package, CPT guidance, 60-day exam tracking, real-time visit visibility,
  **one-hour response**. Minimal referral form + fax/phone. Peer-to-peer tone, zero marketing fluff.
- Separate lanes for EEOICPA, OWCP, and VA referrals.

### 3.8 Mobile-first
- Image-first hero (full composition, no crop, no overlay), copy below on clean background.
- Audience doors stack as full-width cards. Tap-to-call in the header (standing, never floating).
- 17px+ type, 48px targets, one primary action per view, near-zero client JS, fast LCP.

## PART 4 — CHALLENGING THE CURRENT SITE

Honest assessment of what we've built so far, against the "best in America" bar:

1. **Hero (editorial split):** ✅ The structure is right and now world-class in *composition*. ❌ But it
   is **trust-light**: in 5 seconds it shows beauty and a soft positioning line, not *legitimacy*. A
   CMS surveyor or skeptical physician sees no accreditation, no licensure, no named clinician.
   **Fix:** add the credential bar directly under the hero and sharpen the headline from
   "Understanding federal benefits…" to an authority claim ("The care you earned…").
2. **Navigation:** ❌ Currently platform-named (Benefit Navigator / Federal Benefits Library / Trust &
   Sources). That's *features as navigation*. **Fix:** audience/program IA — **Programs** (White
   Card/EEOICPA, OWCP, Veterans), **Services**, **For Physicians**, **About**. The Navigator and
   Library become destinations *inside* Programs/Resources, not the primary menu.
3. **Named clinical leadership:** ❌ Absent above the scroll. The single highest-trust asset (a real
   nurse-founder) is not used. **Fix:** a dedicated leadership block with a real portrait + credentials.
4. **Proof / legitimacy:** ❌ No accreditation, licensure, Medicare cert, or partner proof surfaced.
   **Fix:** credential bar + a proof section + a fully credentialed footer.
5. **Referrer path:** ❌ No referral entry in the primary nav; referrers are a co-equal buyer.
   **Fix:** "For Physicians" in the nav + a dedicated referral band on the homepage.
6. **Copy:** ⚠️ Good and calm, but soft. **Fix:** sharpen to authority + proof; lead with what's
   defensible (DON review, "no cost," program mastery), not adjectives.
7. **Imagery:** ⚠️ The arch is strong but is **shared stock**. **Fix:** plan an exclusive/commissioned
   asset + a real founder portrait before this is truly "the best in America."

**What is already objectively superior and should be preserved:** the warm-neutral palette and
restraint, the editorial split hero composition, the arch motif, the Library + Navigator as the
authority/AEO engine, the YMYL discipline (clinician-reviewed, source-cited), and the staging/SITE_MODE
hygiene.
