Nevada Test Site & NNSS · White Card (EEOICPA)

Worked at the Nevada Test Site? Your White Card can cover care at home.

Whether you remember it as the Nevada Test Site or know it today as the Nevada National Security Site, it is the same place, and the same benefits. When an illness is accepted, it comes with a White Card: lifetime medical benefits at no cost to you, including skilled home health. Here is who is covered, which illnesses qualify, and what the benefit is worth, drawn from the Department of Labor and NIOSH.

01Is the Nevada Test Site covered?

Yes. The Nevada Test Site, now the Nevada National Security Site, is a covered Department of Energy facility under EEOICPA for work from 1951 on. Department of Energy employees and contractor and subcontractor employees who worked there may qualify when a covered illness is linked to that work. The Tonopah Test Range is a covered facility as well.

02Two Special Exposure Cohort periods

For certain periods, the government grants a Special Exposure Cohort. If you worked at the Test Site during one of these windows and later developed one of the 22 specified cancers below, your claim is presumed to be work-related. You do not have to reconstruct your radiation dose.

Cohort period one
Jan 27, 1951 to Dec 31, 1962
At least 250 work days at the Test Site, alone or combined with other covered sites, and monitored or that should have been monitored for radiation.
Cohort period two
Jan 1, 1963 to Dec 31, 1992
At least 250 work days at the Test Site, alone or combined with other covered sites, for Department of Energy, its predecessor agencies, and contractor and subcontractor employees.

Outside these windows, a claim can still be covered. NIOSH reconstructs your radiation dose, and the claim is compensable when the probability that the work caused the cancer is at least 50 percent.

03The 22 specified cancers

For a Special Exposure Cohort member, these cancers are presumed work-related. Onset must be at least two or five years after first exposure, depending on the cancer. This is the federal list, not medical advice; your physician and the Department of Labor determine what applies.

Onset at least 2 years after exposure

  • Leukemia (other than chronic lymphocytic leukemia)
  • Lung cancer (other than in-situ found at autopsy)
  • Bone cancer (primary or secondary)
  • Renal (kidney) cancer

Onset at least 5 years after exposure

  • Multiple myeloma
  • Lymphomas (other than Hodgkin’s disease)
  • Thyroid
  • Male breast · Female breast
  • Esophagus
  • Stomach
  • Pharynx
  • Small intestine
  • Pancreas
  • Bile ducts · Gall bladder
  • Salivary gland
  • Urinary bladder
  • Brain (malignant only)
  • Colon
  • Ovary
  • Liver (except if cirrhosis or hepatitis B is indicated)
04What the White Card covers at home

An accepted claim issues a White Card, the medical-benefits card the Department of Labor honors for care tied to your accepted condition, at no cost to you. The Department of Labor pays the provider directly, and you never receive a bill. Alara provides the home-health side:

Skilled nursing Wound care Physical & occupational therapy Home health aide Caregiver training

05What the benefit is worth

Beyond medical care, EEOICPA can pay compensation. This is general education, not a determination, and Alara does not file or decide claims. The Department of Labor decides every claim.

Part B
$150,000 + lifetime medical

A one-time payment plus lifetime medical benefits for a covered cancer or illness tied to the work. Special Exposure Cohort members with a specified cancer qualify without dose reconstruction.

Part E · for Department of Energy contractor & subcontractor employees
$2,500 for each 1% of impairment

Compensation for whole-body impairment and for wage loss caused by the covered illness, up to a combined maximum of $250,000. Federal Department of Energy employees are not covered under Part E.

A White Card can also grow. A new problem caused by an accepted condition, called a consequential condition, can be added, which can open more care and, under Part E, more impairment compensation.
